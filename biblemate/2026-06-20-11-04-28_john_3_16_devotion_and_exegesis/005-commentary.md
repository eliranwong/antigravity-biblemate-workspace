# Step 005: Commentary Synthesis (John 3:16)

**Persona**: Oxford Bible Scholar  
**Reference**: John 3:16  
**Commentators Synthesized**: John Calvin (Reformed), John Gill (Reformed Baptist), Matthew Henry (Puritan), Albert Barnes (Presbyterian)

---

## 1. John Calvin: The Unmerited Source of Salvation
* **The First Cause**: Calvin emphasizes that Christ opens up the "first cause, and, as it were, the source of our salvation... that no doubt may remain." This first cause is the sovereign, unmerited love of the Heavenly Father, which completely sets aside human merit. Our minds cannot find peace until we rest in this unmerited love.
* **The Emphatic *Monogenēs***: The term "only-begotten" is used to magnify the fervor of God's love. God did not spare His most precious possession, proving how valuable our salvation is to Him.
* **Universal Invitation**: The term "whosoever" (*pas ho pisteuōn*) is used to invite all indiscriminately and remove all excuses from unbelievers. However, Calvin notes that while the invitation is universal, saving faith is not common to all; it is given only to the elect whose eyes God opens.
* **Order of Redemption**: Redemption (reconciliation through the blood of Christ) must logically precede our experience of God's fatherly kindness, because God hates sin and must make atonement before we are restored to life.

---

## 2. John Gill: Breaking Rabbinic Exclusivism
* **Messianic Contrast**: Gill contextualizes John 3:16 within Jesus' discussion with Nicodemus (a Pharisee and ruler of the Jews). He notes that Jesus is directly opposing a commonly received Rabbinic notion: that the Messiah came only to bless Israel and to judge, destroy, or condemn the Gentiles (*the nations of the world*).
* **The Meaning of "The World"**: Gill argues that "the world" (*kosmos*) here refers to the Gentiles (non-Jews) in addition to the Jews. By declaring that God loved *the world*, Jesus reveals that the Messiah's redemptive work extends beyond the borders of Israel to include God's elect among all nations.
* **Evidence of Rabbinic Views**: Gill quotes extensively from the Talmud and Zohar to show that Jewish doctors taught the Messiah would be "sharp to the nations, and tender to Israel," and that the sun of righteousness would burn the idolatrous nations but heal Israel. Jesus' message is a radical, universal pivot: God gave His unique Son so that *whosoever* (Jew or Gentile) believes would not perish.

---

## 3. Matthew Henry: The Greatness of the Gift and Danger of Unbelief
* **The Greatness of God's Love**: Henry marvels at the dimensions of the love of God. God loved a world of rebels who had apostatized from Him. The gift of His Son is unspeakably great because of the dignity of the person given—the only-begotten Son, dearest to the Father.
* **The Mechanism of Faith**: Faith is the instrumental cause. It is not merely a cognitive assent, but a personal trusting and resting in the name of the Son of God.
* **The Danger of Unbelief**: Henry notes that those who reject the Son are "condemned already" (v. 18). This present condemnation is certain and grounded in their refusal of the only remedy. Unbelief is the "great damning sin" because it leaves the sinner under the guilt of all their other transgressions.

---

## 4. Albert Barnes: Benevolence to a Ruined Race
* **The Love of Benevolence**: Barnes makes a critical distinction between two kinds of love:
  1. *Love of Complacency*: Approval of another's conduct or character.
  2. *Love of Benevolence*: Earnest desire for the happiness and welfare of another.
  Barnes asserts that God does not approve of sinful humanity's conduct (which He hates), but He loves them with a love of benevolence, earnestly desiring their salvation.
* **The Hopeless State**: The magnitude of the gift is shown by five circumstances:
  1. The world was in absolute ruin and exposed to wrath.
  2. Humanity was in a completely hopeless condition.
  3. The gift was entirely free, undeserved, and without claim.
  4. The Son was given up to extreme suffering and death on the cross.
  5. The invitation is universal, designed for all who believe.
* **Parental Analogy**: Barnes uses the analogy of a parent who might love a rebellious child and desire their welfare while being strongly opposed to their conduct, showing the depth of God's fatherly heart in sending His Son.
