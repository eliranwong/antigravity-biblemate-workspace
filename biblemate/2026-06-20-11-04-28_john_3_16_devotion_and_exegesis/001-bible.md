## Textual and Translation Analysis

As an Oxford Bible Scholar, several prominent translation decisions and textual variances must be observed:

### 1. The Rendering of *monogenēs* (μονογενής) in John 3:16 & 18
* **KJV**: "only begotten Son"
* **ESV2016 / NASB2020**: "only Son"
* **BSB**: "one and only Son"
* **Scholar's Note**: The Greek adjective *monogenēs* is traditionally translated "only begotten" (from *monos* "only" and *gennao* "to beget"). However, modern lexicography (e.g., BDAG) asserts that the term is derived from *monos* and *genos* ("kind", "class"), yielding the definition of "unique", "one of a kind", or "only". Thus, newer translations favor "only Son" or "one and only Son" to avoid the theological misconception that the Son had a point of origin or creation, preserving His eternal co-existence with the Father.

### 2. "Everlasting Life" vs. "Eternal Life" in John 3:16
* **KJV**: "everlasting life" (in verse 16, though it uses "eternal life" in verse 15)
* **ESV2016 / NASB2020 / BSB**: "eternal life"
* **Scholar's Note**: The Greek phrase is *zōēn aiōnion* (ζωὴν αἰώνιον). While "everlasting" stresses duration (without end), "eternal" emphasizes both quality (a divine, transcendent quality of life belonging to the age to come) and duration. Modern translations uniformly translate *zōēn aiōnion* as "eternal life" in both verses 15 and 16 to maintain consistency and theological depth.

### 3. "Condemn" vs. "Judge" in John 3:17-18
* **KJV / ESV2016 / BSB**: "condemn" / "condemned"
* **NASB2020**: "judge" / "judged"
* **Scholar's Note**: The Greek verb used is *krinō* (κρίνω), which primarily means "to judge", "to decide", or "to pass judgment". In the context of John 3, this judgment leads to condemnation, which is why most translations render it "condemn" (since Jesus contrasts it with "saved" in verse 17). The NASB maintains a literal rendering ("judge"), which highlights the legal, forensic dimension of the division that Christ's presence brings.
