# Step 009: Structural Outline of John 3:14-21

**Persona**: Oxford Bible Scholar  
**Reference Passage**: John 3:14-21  
**Thematic Outline Type**: Exegetical and Structural Breakdown

---

## 1. Segment I: The Necessity of the Exaltation (John 3:14-15)
* **Sub-segment**: John 3:14-15
* **Descriptive Title**: The Wilderness Type and the Necessity of the Crucifixion
* **Structural and Literary Notes**:
  * **Conjunction of Linkage**: The segment begins with "And" (*kai*), linking Jesus' preeminent status as the one who descended from heaven (v. 13) to the historical execution of His mission.
  * **Historical Typology**: Jesus draws a direct comparison (*kathōs... houtōs* - "just as... so must") between Moses lifting up the bronze serpent in Numbers 21:9 and the destiny of the Son of Man.
  * **The Divine Imperative (*dei*)**: The Greek verb *dei* ("must") denotes logical and theological necessity in God's redemptive plan.
  * **Johannine Double Entendre**: The phrase "lifted up" (*hypsōthēnai*) acts as a key literary device. In John, it simultaneously denotes physical crucifixion on the cross and theological exaltation/glorification (cf. John 8:28, 12:32).
  * **Purpose Clause**: The segment ends with a purpose clause (*hina* + subjunctive) defining the purpose of this lifting up: "that everyone believing in Him may have eternal life."

---

## 2. Segment II: The Theological Rationale of Divine Love (John 3:16-17)
* **Sub-segment**: John 3:16-17
* **Descriptive Title**: The Sovereign Sending of the Unique Son
* **Structural and Literary Notes**:
  * **Explanatory Conjunction**: Verse 16 begins with "For" (*gar*), introducing the ultimate theological foundation for the necessity of the Son's lifting up described in verses 14–15.
  * **Climax of Identity**: The speaker transitions from referring to himself as the "Son of Man" (v. 14) to the "only Son" (*ton Huion ton monogenē*), highlighting His unique, pre-existent relationship with the Father.
  * **Antithetical Parallelism**: Verse 17 is structured around a strong contrast:
    * *Negative Thesis*: "For God did not send His Son into the world to judge/condemn the world..."
    * *Positive Purpose*: "...but in order that the world might be saved through Him."
  * **Aorist Action**: The verbs *ēgapēsen* ("loved") and *edōken* ("gave") are both in the aorist tense, designating a completed, decisive historical event (the Incarnation and Atonement).

---

## 3. Segment III: The Forensic Division of Faith (John 3:18)
* **Sub-segment**: John 3:18
* **Descriptive Title**: The Present Verdict of Belief and Unbelief
* **Structural and Literary Notes**:
  * **Realized Eschatology**: This verse presents judgment as a present reality rather than a purely future event. The verdict is active *now* based on one's response to Jesus.
  * **Chiasmus / Parallelism**: The verse is structured symmetrically around two options:
    * **A**: The one believing in Him (*ho pisteuōn eis auton*) $\rightarrow$ is not judged (*ou krinetai*).
    * **B**: The one not believing (*ho de mē pisteuōn*) $\rightarrow$ is judged already (*ēdē kekritai*).
  * **The Ground of Verdict**: The perfect indicative *kekritai* ("has been judged already") denotes an abiding, completed state of condemnation, justified by the final clause: "because he has not believed in the name of the unique Son of God."

---

## 4. Segment IV: The Moral Manifestation of Light and Darkness (John 3:19-21)
* **Sub-segment**: John 3:19-21
* **Descriptive Title**: The Metaphor of the Light and the Separation of Deeds
* **Structural and Literary Notes**:
  * **Thematic Inclusio**: The metaphor of "light" (*phōs*) coming into the world and the conflict with "darkness" (*skotos*) mirrors the Prologue of the Gospel (John 1:4-9, 11).
  * **Parallel Metaphorical Contrast**:
    * *The Wicked*: "Everyone doing evil (*pas ho phaula prassōn*) hates the light... lest his deeds should be exposed."
    * *The Righteous*: "The one doing truth (*ho de poiōn tēn alētheian*) comes to the light... that his deeds may be clearly seen as having been wrought in God."
  * **Moral/Ethical Focus**: The shift from the theological category of "faith" to the ethical categories of "deeds" (*erga*) shows that unbelief has a moral, rather than merely intellectual, root. Men loved darkness because their deeds were evil (v. 19).
